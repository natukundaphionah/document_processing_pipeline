Greeting one person, responding and enquiring about one's wellbeing

This section discusses how to greet one person and how to talk about wellbeing when speaking to one person.

Enquiring about one's wellbeing (one person)

This is how we enquire about the wellbeing of others. As we've done previously, we're giving you all the common ways to ask this question to one person.

- Unjani? (How are you?)
- Unjani Themba? (How are you, Themba?)
- Unjani Tata? (How are you, Father?); or
- Kunjani? (Howzit?)

Response

These are the common ways of responding to an enquiry about your wellbeing. The responses below only answer this question in the singular.

- Ndiphilile enkosi, unjani wena? (I'm well, thank you. How are you?)
- Ndiyaphila enkosi, unjani wena / kunjani kuwe? (I'm well, thank you. How are you?)
- Ndikhona enkosi, unjani wena? (I am well, thank you. How are you?)
- Hayi, ndikhona enkosi, wena? (No, I'm well, thank you, and you?)
- Hayi, ndiphilile enkosi. Wena? (No, I'm well, thank you. And you?)

Further response

In this situation, you've just asked someone how they are and they've responded to you. Then, they've reciprocated and asked how you are. Below is a list of how you'd respond. As always, we're giving you various options.

- Hayi, nam ndiphilile enkosi. (No, I'm also well, thank you.)
- Ndiphilile enkosi. (I'm well, thank you.)
- Ndikhona enkosi. (I'm well, thank you.)
- Hayi ndikhona enkosi. (No, I'm well, thank you.)

Greeting more than one person, responding to and enquiring about the wellbeing of many

It is important to note that, in isiXhosa, there's a difference between how you'd greet one person and how to greet more than one person. IsiXhosa is not like other languages where the same greeting phrase can be used to greet one, two and more people. It's also important to note that there's a difference when you enquire about the wellbeing of one person and when you enquire about the wellbeing of various people.

Greeting more people than one person

Below is an example of greeting more than one person. You would greet more than one

person in gatherings like business meetings, similar to the one shown in Figure 2 below.



Molweni! (Hello!)

If the person greeting two or more people was alone, the possible responses would be:

- Molo;
- Molo Sipho; or
- Ewe!

If two or more people are being greeted by more than one other person, the response to the initial greeting will be Molweni.

## Enquiring after the wellbeing of two or more people

We enquire after the wellbeing of two or more people in the following two ways:

- Ninjani? (How are you?) or
- Kunjani? (How are you? – less formal)

&gt; **NOTE**
&gt;
&gt; 'Ni' is the plural, used when there are two people or more.

## The response

Common responses to an enquiry like Ninjani are as follows:

- Siphilile enkosi, nina? (We are well, thank you, and you?)
- Siphilile, enkosi ninjani nina? (We are well, thank you, how are you?)
- Siyaphila, enkosi, nina? (We are well, thank you, how are you?)
- Hayi, siphilile, enkosi ninjani nina? (No, we are well, thank you, how are you?)
- Hayi, sikhona enkosi, kunjani kuni? (No, we are well, thank you, how are you?).

Response to the above

In this situation, you've just asked two or more people how they are and they've responded to you. Then, they've reciprocated and asked how you are. This is how you'd respond. As always, we're giving you various options.

- Hayi nathi siyaphila enkosi. (No, we are also well, thank you.)
- Siphilile enkosi. (We are well, thank you.)
- Sikhona enkosi. (We are well, thank you.)
- Hayi sikhona enkosi. (No, we are well, thank you.)

We now move on to discuss how to introduce yourself by name, surname and clan name. It is culturally important for you to know how to do this – it is ubuntu to introduce yourself fully and to know the person you are speaking with.

Introducing yourself by name, surname and clan name

In this section, you will learn about introducing yourself by name, surname and clan name.

Name

This is how you would introduce yourself by your first name. You would be asked the following question:

- Ungubani igama lakho? (What is your name?)

Your response would be one of the following:

- NdinguNomsa. (I'm Nomsa.)
- Igama lam nguLizo. (My name is Lizo.)
- Mna ndinguSipho. (I'm Sipho.)
- Elam nguSipho. (Mine is Sipho.)

Surname

This is how you would introduce yourself by your surname. You would be asked the following question:

- Ungubani ifani yakho? (What is your surname?)

Your response would be one of the following:

- NdinguLoliwe ifani yam. (My surname is Lolilwe.)
- Ifani yam nguNtaba, eyakho? (My surname is Ntaba, and yours?)
- Eyam nguMlilo. (Mine is Mlilo.)
- NguMlilo. (It's Mlilo.)

Clan name

Clan names are special. They are family names and are often considered more important than surnames.

Now that you understand what clan names are, it is time to learn how to introduce yourself by your clan name. You would be asked one of the following questions:

- Ungumni? (What is your clan name?)
- Ngubani isiduko sakho? (What is your clan name?)

You would answer in one of the following ways:
- NdinguZizi. (I am from the Zizi clan.)
- Mna ndinguJola. (I am from the Jola clan.)

After meeting someone, you should show your appreciation for meeting them and wish them well.

Listed below are examples of how to show your appreciation of someone you've just met:
- Ndiyavuya ukukwazi. (I am pleased to meet you.)
- Ndiyavuya ukukwazi Sipho. (I am pleased to meet you, Sipho.)
- Nam ndiyavuya ukukwazi. (I am also pleased to meet you.)
- Nam ndiyavuya ukukwazi Lizo. (I am also pleased to meet you, Lizo.)

The following are examples of 'saying goodbye' in various situations and to various numbers of people:
- Sala(ni) / hamba(ni) kakuhle. (Goodbye.)
- Sala kakuhle (to one person). (Stay well).
- Salani / Nisale kakuhle (to two or more people). (Stay well.)
- Hamba kakuhle (to one person). (Go well.)
- Hambani / Nihambe kakuhle (to two or more people). (Go well.)
- Enkosi. (Thank you.)

## Different Forms of Greetings and Basic Conversational Phrases

In this section of the lesson we discuss different forms of greetings. We will discuss formal greetings, casual greetings and specifically how to greet older people.

## Formal greetings

We will now be looking at formal ways of greeting people you know, such as friends, school mates and colleagues at work.

## Casual greetings

Below are some examples of casual greetings:

- Eita Sbari (referring to a friend in this situation).

Greeting older people

It is important to greet older people in a very respectful, dignified manner that is quite different from how you would greet your friend. It's also important to have a closing phrase when you speak to an older person, like Mama, Tata or Tatomkhulu. As we mentioned previously in this study guide, the younger person is expected to greet first.

Here are some examples of how to greet older people:

Molo Tata.
Molo Mntwana wam (my child).
Unjani Makhulu?
Ndiphilile enkosi Mzukulwana wam (my grandchild).
Unjani wena?
Nam ndiphilile enkosi Makhulu.
Bhotani Malume.
Bhota Mtshana (my sisters' child).
Molo Dadobawo (sister to one's father).
Ungubani igama lakho Ntombi yam (my girl)?
NguLiziwe Bhuti.
Igama lam nguNonceba Mama.
Lamnandi eli gama (beautiful name).
Enkosi kakhulu Tata.
Ndiyavuya ukukwazi Ntombi.
Nam Makhulu ndiyavuya ukukwazi.

# Communication about where one stays, with whom he or she lives and the address

This subsection explores the communication around addresses and living arrangements. Below are examples of common questions that you may be asked about where you live and the possible answers to each of them.

Uhlala phi? (Where do you live?)

Ndihlala eThekwini. (I live in Durban.)
Ndihlala kwaMashu. (I live in Mashu.)
Ndihlala eBhayi. (I live in Port Elizabeth.)

Wena uhlala phi? (And you, where do you live?)

- Nam ndihlala kwaMashu. (I also live in Mashu.)
Ndihlala eRichards Bay. (I live in Richards Bay.)
Mna ndihlala eNkandla. (I live in Nkandla.)

Below are examples of how to tell people your address (idilesi):

Uhlala ndawoni eDurban? (Where do you live in Durban?)

- Ndihlala KwaMashu. (I live in Mashu)
- Ndihlala esikolweni. (I live at school.)
- Ndihlala ekhampasini. (I live in the campus.)

Nihlala ndawoni eBhayi? (Where do you live in Port Elizabeth?)
- Sihlala kwaZakhele. (We live in Zakhele.)

Uhlala kwesiphi isitalato? (What street do you live in?)
- Ndihlala eStemele isitalato. (I live in Stemele street.)
- Uhlala kwanombolo bani? (What is your house number?)
- Ndihlala kwanombolo 84. (I live at number 84.)

Nihlala kwesiphi isitalato? (What street do you live in?)
- Sihlala eSheya isitalato. (We live in Sheya street.)

Nihlala kwanombolo bani? (What is your house number?)
- Sihlala kwanombolo 101. (We live at number 101.)
- Uhlala nabani? (Who do you live with?)
- Nihlala nabani? (Who do you live with?)
- Ndi-(si)hlala ndodwa (sodwa). (I (we) live alone.)
- Ndi-(si)hlala noBheki. (I (we) live with Bheki.)
- Ndi-(si)hlala nabazali bam (bethu). (I (we) live with my (our) parents.)
- Ndi-(si)hlala nosapho lwam (lwethu). (I (we) live with my (our) family.)
- Ndi-(si)hlala nabantwana basekhaya. (I (we) live with my siblings.)
- Ndi-(si)hlala nomhlobo wam (wethu). (I live with my friend / we live with our friend.)
- Sihlala nabahlobo bethu. (We live with our friends.)
