# Introduction

Congratulations (makorokoto) for taking the first step to learn Shona. This book is an ideal introduction to Shona language and is designed for adults and children learning at home or in class. It covers the basics of the language required in everyday situations. Complicated grammar rules are not covered.

## Shona Language (Shona or chiShona)

Shona is a native language to the majority of Zimbabweans. It is also spoken in some parts of Zambia, Mozambique, Botswana and South Africa. In Zimbabwe, Shona is spoken in five dialects: chiKaranga; chiZezuru; chiManyika; chiKorekore; and chiNdau. Speakers of these dialects can understand each other very well although some words and phrases cause confusion in some situations. To keep the learning process simple, this book has not covered the differences between dialects.

## Learn Shona

The structure of this book encourages step-by-step learning. The key features of the book are:

- Step-by-step presentation of the new language.
- Simple sentence structures and rules are presented with examples.
- There are practice questions at the end of the chapters to review the concepts learned.
- Answers to the practice questions are given at the end of the book.
- There is progression in the learning material.
- Complicated grammar rules have not been covered.

## How to use the Book

- Work your way through the material chapter by chapter.
- Practise pronouncing the new words by reference to the pronunciation guide. If you are in contact with Shona speakers, practise with them.
- Complete the practice questions and compare your answers to the answers at the back.
- Take note of the simple rules introduced in each chapter.
- Finally, dedicate time every day or every week to consolidate your learning.

# Pronunciation Guide

## Vowels

The vowel sounds are as described below.

A ah - same sound as **a** in the following words: arm; Africa; pasta.
E eh - same sound as **e** in the following words: egg; bed; end.
I ee - same sound as **i** in the words: India; king; inn.
O oh - same sound as the **o** in the words: old; on; off.
U oo - same sound as **u** or **oo** in the words: June; food; mood.

To make the **u** in English words such as uniform and united, this is written as **yu**.

Also note the following:

1. All Shona words end with a vowel.
2. The vowel sounds do not change and will always have the sound described above.
3. If a vowel is followed by another vowel, each vowel is pronounced separately. For example **amai** (mother) is pronounced **a-ma-i** (ah-mah-ee). Another example **haapo** (he/she is absent) is pronounced **ha-ah-poh**.
4. Shona does not have accented vowels.

## Consonants

|  B | When the **b** is followed by a vowel (a,e,i,o,u), this has a different sound from a **b** in English. To make the correct Shona sound, bring the lips together as if you are about to make a **p** sound but do not push out the air. Common Shona words include **baba** (father), **bata** (touch), **Mugabe**.  |
| --- | --- |
|  Bh | Same sound as the **b** in English. For example, **bhuku** is pronounced book-oo.  |
|   | There are other sounds derived from **b** such as **bv**, **bw**, where the v & w have a sound. Example words are: **ibva** (go away); **bveni** (baboon); **ibwe** (rock).  |
|  D | When followed by a vowel, this has a softer sound when compared to the **d** in English. To make the correct Shona sound, let your tongue have a softer touch when making the English d sound. Common words include: **rudo** (love); **dare** (meeting); **dova** (dew).  |
|  Dh | This has a sound similar to a **d** in English. An example is **dhadha** (duck).  |
|  V | When followed by a vowel, this has a different sound to the **v** in English. To make the correct Shona sound, do not push out the air out of your mouth when making the English **v** sound. Common Shona words include: **vana** (children); **vakomana** (boys), **verenga** (read).  |
|  Vh | This is the normal **v** sound in English.  |

The **v** in Shona is also used in combination with other letters resulting in “whistling” sounds. These sounds are usually difficult for native English speakers to pronounce. One example is the name **Tsvangirai**, former Prime Minister of Zimbabwe. The English media has been pronouncing this as **Changirai**. The **tsv** sound is a whistling sound made by combining **ts** and **v** sounds and is different from the **ch** sound widely used in English media. Other combinations are:

Sv svosve (ants); svika (arrive); svova (abscond).
Tsv tsvaira (sweep); mutsvairo (broom); mutsvene (holy).
Zv zvinhu (things); zvuva (pull); zvombo (weapons).

There are other less used combinations such as “nzv” e.g. nzvimbo (place); “dzv” e.g. dzvinyu (lizard).

The other sounds with examples from English words are as follows:

Ch charm. The **ch** never has **k** sound in Shona.
F father
G girl. The **g** never has a **j** sound
H hotel
J jelly. The **j** never has a **y** sound.
K kite.
L Letter is not used in Shona. In imported words, **l** is replaced by **r**.
M milk
N November
P paper
Q not used in Shona writing. The **q** sound is written **kw**.
R radio
S salt
T tea
W woman
X not used in Shona
Y yes
Z Zimbabwe

The consonant sounds are consistent and pronounced the same when used in different positions in a word. For example in English, the first **g** in the word “garage” has different sound from the second **g**. This does not happen in Shona. Instead, the word garage is written **garaji**.

# 2. Greetings

## Greetings Etiquette

The younger person is expected to initiate the greetings.

A hat should be taken off during greetings or if you are entering a house.

As a sign of respect, the “How are you?” should be accompanied by a polite slow hand-clap.

## Example dialogues

### In the morning

|  You | Mangwanani baba (Good morning father)  |
| --- | --- |
|  Father | Mangwanani mwanangu (Good morning my child)  |
|  You | Mamuka sei? (Did you sleep well?)  |
|  Father | Ndamuka kana wamukawo? (I slept well if you slept well?)  |
|  You | Ndamuka hangu (I slept well)  |

### In the afternoon and in the evening

|  You | Masikati amai (Good afternoon mother) or Manheru amai (Good evening mother)  |
| --- | --- |
|  Mother | Masikati mwanangu (Good afternoon my child) or Manheru mwanangu (Good evening my child)  |
|  You | Maswera sei? (How was your day?)  |
|  Mother | Ndaswera kana waswerawo? (My day was good; and yours?)  |
|  You | Ndaswera hangu (My day was good).  |

### After some period of separation

|  You | Ndisvikewo ambuya? (May I arrive, grandmother?)  |
| --- | --- |
|  Grandmother | Titambire muzukuru (Welcome, grandchild)  |

This is followed by a handshake or a hug. If you are carrying bags, there will be offers to carry your bags for the remainder of the journey however short. Once everyone sits down and settle, the greetings proceed as follows.

|  You | Makadii ambuya? (How are you grandmother?)  |
| --- | --- |
|  Grandmother | Ndiripo hangu kana wakadiiwo muzukuru (I am well; and how are you grandchild?)  |
|  You | Ndiripo hangu (I am well)  |
|  Grandmother | Wasiya vamwe vari sei? (How is everyone you left behind?)  |
|  You | Varipo havo, vanga vachikufarisai (They are well; they pass their greetings to you)  |

## Addressing People

It is disrespectful to call people older than you by their first name. In a business environment, you address the adults by their surnames as follows:

Mr Moyo VaMoyo
Mrs Moyo Mai Moyo
Miss Moyo Muzvare Moyo

In informal environment, if an adult has children, you call them as follows:

Baba Tari This means Tari's father. Tari is the name of his eldest child.
Mai Tari This means Tari's mother.

If they do not have children or you do not know the name of the first child, then the use of the formal Va (Mr) or Mai (Mrs) will suffice.

For siblings and cousins, mukoma is used to show respect to the boys and sisi is used for the girls.

## 5. Simple Sentences: In the First Person

Simple sentence construction takes the subject-verb-object (SVO) form. This chapter shows you how to make simple sentences in the first person narrative.

### New words

imba (sing) – a verb. Kuimba (to sing or singing)
mari (money) – an object.

### Present Tense

|  English | Shona | Examples  |
| --- | --- | --- |
|  I am (doing something) | Ndiri ku+verb | Ndiri kuimba (I am singing)  |
|  I am (something) | Ndiri mu... | Ndiri muimbi (I am a singer)  |
|  I have (something) | Ndine + object | Ndine mari (I have some money)  |
|  I do (something) | Ndino ... | Ndinoimba (I sing)  |

### Past Tense and Future Tense

|  English | Shona | Examples  |
| --- | --- | --- |
|  I did/I have done (something) | Ndaka + verb | Ndakaimba (I sang)  |
|  I have (just done something) | Nda+verb | Ndaimba (I have just sung)  |
|  I will (be doing something) | Ndicha+verb | Ndichaimba (I will sing)  |
|  I had (something) | Ndaiva ne+object | Ndaiva nemari (I had some money)  |

### Plural

To change the above into plural, replace the “nd” with “t”. See table below.

|  English | Shona | Examples  |
| --- | --- | --- |
|  We are (doing something) | Tiri ku+verb | Tiri kuimba (We are singing)  |
|  We are (something) | Tiri va+verb | Tiri vaimbi (We are singers)  |
|  We have (something) | Tine+object | Tine mari (We have money)  |
|  We do (something) | Tino... | Tinoimba (We sing)  |
|  We did/We have done | Taka+verb | Takaimba (We sang)  |
|  We have (just done something) | Ta+verb | Taimba (We have just sung)  |
|  We will (be doing something) | Ticha+verb | Tichaimba (We will sing)  |
|  We had (something) | Taiva ne+object | Taiva nemari (We had some money)  |

# 7. Simple Sentences: In the Third Person

In Shona, third person pronouns are gender neutral. The same word is used for "he" and for "she". The only difference is whether you need to show respect to the person you are referring to or not.

## Present Tense

|  English | Shona | Example  |
| --- | --- | --- |
|  He/she is (when referring to someone you do not need to show respect, e.g., your young brother) | Ari | Ari kutaura (He is talking/She is talking)  |
|  He/she is (when referring to someone you respect, e.g., your mother) | Vari | Vari kutaura (He is talking/She is talking)  |
|  They are | Vari | Vari kutaura (They are talking)  |
|  He/she has (when referring to someone you do not need to show respect, e.g., your young brother) | Ane | Ane bhuku (She has a book/He has a book)  |

10. Possessives
There are different classes of possessives. The ones given in this chapter are by no means exhaustive but they will allow you to get by in most situations.

In English, you say "My book" and in Shona you say "Bhuku rangu" (book mine). To determine the possessives, first you have to determine if the following should be used: wa...; va...; ya...; dza...; ra...; etc.

The following apply when referring relatives except where the word starts with mu...

|  English | Shona  |
| --- | --- |
|  My father | Baba vangu  |
|  Our father | Baba vedu  |
|  His/Her father | Baba vake  |
|  His/Her father | Baba vavo (when showing respect)  |
|  Their father | Baba vavo  |
|  Your father | Baba vako  |
|  Your father | Baba venyu (when showing respect)  |

The following work with most words starting with mu...

|  English | Shona  |
| --- | --- |
|  My teacher | Mudzidzisi wangu  |
|  Our teacher | Mudzidzisi wedu  |
|  His/Her teacher | Mudzidzisi wake  |
|  His/Her teacher | Mudzidzisi wavo (when showing respect)  |
|  Their teacher | Mudzidzisi wavo  |
|  Your teacher | Mudzidzisi wako  |
|  Your teacher | Mudzidzisi wenyu (when showing respect)  |

The following work with most objects.

|  English | Shona  |
| --- | --- |
|  My money | Mari yangu  |
|  Our money | Mari yedu  |
|  His/Her money | Mari yake  |
|  His/Her money | Mari yavo (when showing respect)  |
|  Their money | Mari yavo  |
|  Your money | Mari yako  |
|  Your money | Mari yenyu (when showing respect)  |

Notice how the ending changes: ...ngu (my); ...du (our); ...ke(his/her); ...vo (their); ...ko (your); and ...nyu (your).

15. Numbers

English numbers are adopted widely so you do not have to worry about translating numbers into Shona. Telephone numbers, prices, measurements and mathematical calculations are done using English numbers.

Below is how you count from 1 to 20 in Shona.

One (1)  Motsi
Two (2)  Piri
Three (3)  Tatu
Four (4)  China
Five (5)  Shanu
Six (6)  Tanhatu
Seven (7)  Nomwe
Eight (8)  Sere
Nine (9)  Pfumbamwe
Ten (10)  Gumi
Eleven (11)  Gumi neimwe
Twelve (12)  Gumi nembiri
Thirteen (13)  Gumi nenhatu
Fourteen (14)  Gumi neina
Fifteen (15)  Gumi neshanu
Sixteen (16)  Gumi nenhanhatu
Seventeen (17)  Gumi nenomwe
Eighteen (18)  Gumi nesere
Nineteen (19)  Gumi nepfumbamwe
Twenty (20)  Makumi maviri
Thirty (30)  Makumi matatu
Hundred (100)  Zana
Thousand (1000)  Churu

Plural for gumi (10) is makumi (tens). Plural for zana (100) is mazana (hundreds). Plural for churu (1,000) is zvuru (thousands)

General rules

1. Numbers from 11 to 99 are referenced to 10 (gumi). For example, twelve is gumi nembiri (10 and 2) while twenty is makumi maviri (2 tens) and fifty four is makumi mashanu neina (5 tens and 4).

2. Numbers from 101 to 999 are referenced to 100 (zana) and 10 (gumi). For example 220 is mazana maviri nemakumi maviri (2 hundreds and 2 tens)

3. Numbers from 1,001 to 99,999 are referenced to 1,000 (churu), 100 (zana) and 10 (gumi). For example 2,220 is zvuru zviviri nemazana maviri nemakumi maviri. This gets complicated as the numbers get bigger.

# Appendix 4 - Food and Drink

The staple food in Zimbabwe is sadza, which is a paste made from mealie-meal (upfu) and water (mvura). Sadza is usually served with any of the following: meat and vegetables (nyama nemuriwo); vegetables only (muriwo); sour milk (mukaka); eggs (mazai); meat and gravy (nyama nemuto); vegetable in peanut butter (muriwo une dovi).

# Appendix 6 – Time, Days and Months

In the old days, a day was dedicated each week for resting and it was a taboo to work in the fields on that day. It used to be any designated day (commonly Friday (Chishanu)) and this is still being observed in villages. Since the introduction of Christianity, the day of worship (Saturday or Sunday) was added. In villages, they now observe two rest days while in towns a single day of rest (Saturday or Sunday) is observed for religious reasons.
